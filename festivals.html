Festivals of Jagtial

A fieldwork-based digital archive of festival narratives, ritual practices, regional vocabulary, speaker accounts, English renderings, and ethnographic research notes from Jagtial, Telangana.

Corpus: 10 documented festival and life-cycle traditions.

Corpus Entry 01: Theertham / Jaathara

Festival Name: Theertham / Jaathara (Regional Folk Fair and Temple Festival)

Speaker: Chandraiah

Age: 88

Occupation: Farmer

Original Speaker Account

అప్పుడు పైసక్కొబ్బరికాయ అన్నట్టు ఒక కొబ్బరికాయ తెచ్చి ఇంట్లోల్లందరు పెద్దమనిషి ఎవలుంటే ఆళ్ళని మొక్కి కొట్టుమందురు, అందరు మంచిగుండాలె పంటలు మంచిగ పండాలే అని కొట్టి మొక్కుదురు. ఒక్కపొద్దు ఉందురు. సుట్టాలు ఇంటికత్తురు. ఆల్లకు మంచిగ అండివెట్టి అర్సుకుందుము. పాతదోస్తులం కలిసి తిని తాగి మాట్లాడుకుందుం.

English Translation

Back then, as the saying goes, a coconut cost a paisa. We would bring a coconut home, bow to the eldest person in the family, and ask them to break it. We would pray that everyone should remain well and that the crops should yield well. We would observe “okkapoddu” a form of fasting. Relatives would visit our home, and we would prepare food for them and talk about each other's well-being. Old friends would gather, eat, drink, and talk together.

Festival Practices

The speaker's account mentions several practices associated with the festival: breaking a coconut through the senior member of the family, praying for the well-being of family members and good crop yields, observing “okkapoddu”, receiving relatives, preparing festive meals, and gathering with old friends.

Local Terminology

పైసక్కొబ్బరికాయ (Paisaakkobbarikaaya): An expression used by the speaker while recalling the financial situations in the earlier times.

పెద్దమనిషి (Pedda Manishi): The senior or elder person in the family who performs or leads the ritual act described by the speaker.

ఒక్కపొద్దు (Okkapoddu): A fasting practice involving limited food intake, often associated with taking one meal during the day.

అర్సుకుందుము (Arsukundumu): A regional expression referring to receiving people warmly and inquiring about their well-being.

Cultural Interpretation and Change

Chandraiah's account presents the festival as both a ritual and social occasion. The prayer for good health and successful crops reflects the close relationship between festival observance and agricultural life. The involvement of the family elder in breaking the coconut also indicates the role of senior family members in domestic ritual practices.

The account further emphasizes the social dimension of the festival. Relatives visited one another, festive meals were prepared, and old friends gathered to eat, drink, and talk. The speaker's reference to the low historical price of a coconut also introduces an element of economic memory, contrasting his recollection of earlier times with present-day conditions.

Corpus Entry 02: Puttentikalu

Festival Name: Putthentikalu (Ritual Tonsure / First Haircutting Ceremony)

Speaker: Vajramma

Age: 80

Occupation: Agricultural Labourer

Original Speaker Account:

పస్టు పుట్టేన్టికలు పెట్టుకొని, పోశవ్వ తల్లి కాడ బోనందీసి ఎములాడ దేవుని దగ్గరికి మొక్కులున్నోల్లు ఏం చేస్తురంటే ఇంత గడ్డి వెట్టుకొని ఎడ్లబండ్లె, ఉప్పు పప్పు బోళ్ళు బొకేలు బట్టలు సదురుకొని పొద్దూక పొయ్యి అయ్యగారింటి కాడ నిద్రయేసి పొద్దుగాల మబ్బుల ముడిటికి లేశి గుల్లెకి పొయ్య మోక్కులు అప్పయేప్పి బెల్లం పంచి, బద్ది పోశమ్మ కాడ కోన్ని కోసి బోనం అండుకొని తిని తాగి ఇంటికత్తురు.

English Translation: First, the birth-hair (Putthentikalu) is clipped, and Bonam (a ritual food offering) is offered at Mother Poshamma’s shrine. Those who have pending vows to fulfill at the Vemulawada temple (Emulaada Devudu) pack a bundle of grass into their bullock carts, along with salt, lentils, cooking vessels (Bollu Bokeelu), and clothes. Traveling until dusk (Poddooka), they stay the night at the priest’s (Ayyagaaru) house. Waking up in the early hours before dawn (Mabbula Muditiki), around three o'clock, they proceed to the temple, fulfill their vows (Mukkulu Appaeppi), and distribute jaggery. Then, at the Baddi Poshamma shrine, they sacrifice a goat/fowl (Koni Kosi), cook the Bonam, eat, drink, and return home.

Festival Practices: Child's first ritual haircut; offering cooked rice pot (Bonam) to the local goddess Poshamma; packing bullock carts with self-sustaining provisions (fodder, salt, pulses, cooking utensils); nocturnal lodging at a Brahmin priest's house (Ayyagaari illu); pre-dawn temple darshan at 3:00 AM; distribution of jaggery (Bellam) as prasadam; animal sacrifice (Koni Kosi) at the peripheral shrine of Baddi Poshamma followed by a sacrificial feast.

Local Terminology:

Bollu Bokeelu (బోళ్ళు బొకేలు): A specific regional double-noun referring comprehensively to kitchen utensils and brass/earthen pots required for outdoor camp cooking.

Poddooka (పొద్దూక): Sunset / the precise hour when the sun drops below the horizon.

Mabbula Muditiki (మబ్బుల ముడిటికి): Literally "the third hour in the clouds," meaning 3:00 AM, capturing the traditional pre-modern tracking of nocturnal time.

Mokkulu Appaeppi (మోక్కులు అప్పఎప్పి): Literally "surrendering or returning the vows," a distinct transactional expression for fulfilling a debt owed to a deity.

Baddi Poshamma (బద్దీ పోశమ్మ): A peripheral village deity shrine located near the boundaries of the major Sanskritized Shaivite temple complex at Vemulawada.

Cultural Interpretation: Vajramma’s account highlights the syncretic, multi-layered religious universe of agrarian subalterns. The pilgrimage bridges two vastly different religious traditions: the Sanskritized temple of Emulaada (Sri Raja Rajeshwara Swamy, a form of Shiva) and the localized, non-Sanskritized village goddess Poshamma. For an agricultural laborer, fulfilling a vow is structured as a literal transactional settlement (Mukkulu Appaeppi). The inclusion of "a bunch of grass in the carts" showcases an intimate multi-species care system where the working bullocks' nutrition is prioritized alongside the family's provisions during travel.

Variation and Change: This narrative archives a completely vanished travel infrastructure. The meticulous packing of salt, pulses, and kitchen vessels highlights an era of total self-reliance during travel, necessitated by a lack of commercial hotels and caste-segregated dining options. Staying at the Ayyagaaru (priest) house implies a traditional patron-client network (Jajmani-adjacent structures) that has largely disintegrated in contemporary commercialized temple tourism.

Research Note: This text is a prime example of folk cartographic memory. It details how marginal communities navigated sacred landscapes by relying on spatial checkpoints (traveling till Poddooka, sleeping at the priest’s house, entering the temple at Muditiki). It provides excellent raw data for a doctoral dissertation tracking the intersection of subaltern mobility, domestic labor, and non-Brahminical ritual synthesis.

Corpus Entry 03: Ogaadi

Festival Name: Ogaadi (Ugadi / Telugu New Year)

Speaker: Ramesh

Age: 53

Occupation: Farmer

Original Speaker Account:

కొత్త సంవస్రం కొత్తగ సాగువాటు గావలె, పొద్దుగాల దుర్ముహూర్తం సోర్రకముందు పిడుకల కుంచె(పాటువ) పెట్టి సాగువాటు అవ్వుదురు. కొన్ని రోజులు చేన్ల సాగువాటు అవ్వుడు ఉండే. మబ్బుల లేచి దొడ్డికి పొయ్యి, ఉడుకుడుకు నీళ్ళు కాగవెట్టినవలె తానం చేసి బొట్టు వెట్టుకొని కంకునాలు కట్టుకొని, పసువు కుంకుమ చేతవట్టుకొని పొయ్యి, ఆ చీకట్లే నాలుగు తుమ్మచెట్లు దొరికిచ్చుకొని కొట్టి, అటువారేసి ఎడ్లను దొరికిచ్చుకొని, ఇంత బొట్టువెట్టి, ఇంత సున్నం పసువుపూసి నాగలి, పాలేకు బొట్టువెట్టి. భూమికి మొక్కి, నాగలి మేడల మీదవెట్టి, దొత్తేలు కట్టి ఒక కొండ్ర కలిపి నాగాలిడిషి తూరుపు తిరిగి దండం పెట్టి, ఎడ్ల పెయ్యి కడిగి, ఆడ కట్టేసి, ఇంటికచ్చి బూరెలు అయ్యినంక పొయ్యి ఎడ్లకి బొట్టువెట్టి, కొమ్ములకి అయిందం రాసి రెండు, మూడు, నాలు బూరెలు పెడుదురు. రూపాయి కరసు వెట్టపోదురు – కర్ర్సువెడ్తే ఈ సమత్రం మోత్తం కరసు ఐతయట అని పెట్ట పోదురు.

English Translation: For the New Year, a fresh agricultural cultivation season (Saaguvaatu) must begin. In the morning, before the inauspicious hour (Durmuhoortham) enters, they establish the Pidukala Kunche (a ritual pillar of dried cow-dung cakes) and initiate the plow. In earlier times, this first plowing would continue in the fields for a few consecutive days. Waking up before dawn, going to the cattle backyard, bathing in boiling hot water, applying a tilak, and tying protective threads (Kankanalu), one takes vermilion and turmeric in hand. In that pitch darkness, they go out, locate and cut down four babool (Tumma) branches. Throwing them aside, they bring out the oxen, apply a tilak, and smear them with lime and turmeric. They apply a tilak to the sharecropper/plowman (Bhaagaali Paalae), bow down to the Earth, set the plow upon the yoke, tie the leather straps (Dotthelu), plow a single furrow (Kondra), unyoke the plow, turn toward the east, and prostrate. After washing the oxen’s bodies and tying them up there, they return home. Once the festive sweet buns (Burelu) are ready, they return to the oxen, apply a tilak, smear oil (Ayindam) onto their horns, and feed them two, three, or four sweet buns. They would strictly refuse to spend even a single rupee on this day—believing that if you incurred expenses today, the entire year (Ee Samaatram) would be consumed by continuous expenses.

Festival Practices: Ritual initiation of seasonal plowing (Saaguvaatu) at dawn; construction of the Pidukala Kunche ritual stack; tying apotropaic cords (Kankanalu); gathering babool wood in total darkness; painting working oxen with turmeric, vermilion, and slaked lime; ritual acknowledging and blessing the tenant-partner or farm laborer (Bhaagaali Paalae); worshiping the personified soil (Bhoomi); ritually cutting open the first single agricultural furrow (Kondra); eastern astral prostration; applying oil (Ayindam) to bovine horns and feeding them domestic festive confectioneries (Burelu); observing a total cash expenditure embargo for 24 hours.

Local Terminology:

Saaguvaatu (సాగువాటు): The inaugural, ritualistic turning of the soil that legally and spiritually marks the opening of the annual cultivation cycle.

Pidukala Kunche / Paatuva (పిడుకల కుంచె): A traditional arrangement of dung-cakes used as a mini-hearth or altar during agrarian New Year dawn invocations.

Bhaagaali Paalae (బాగాలి పాలె): A sharecropping partner or long-term attached agricultural worker who operates under mutual land-labor risk splits.

Dotthelu (దొత్తేలు): Heavy-duty leather straps or specialized ropes used to secure the plow share firmly to the wooden yoke.

Kondra (కొండ్ర): A single, unbroken straight line carved into the field by a moving plow.

Ayindam (అయిందం): Castor oil or a heavy lipid mixture applied to cattle horns to prevent cracking and parasitical infestation.

Ee Samaatram (ఈ సమాత్రం): A highly distinct Telangana dialectical variant meaning "this entire year's duration."

Cultural Interpretation: Ramesh’s account shows that Ogaadi is fundamentally an production-oriented ritual designed to secure agricultural luck, rather than just a celebration of a calendar shift. The entire sequence is structurally designed to handle risk. Tying Kankanalu protect the body, while cutting the Kondra establishes control over nature. The ritual bowing to the Bhaagaali Paalae reveals how class and labor relations are managed: the farm owner must ritually honor and feed the laboring body that drives his yield. Finally, the strict cash embargo showcases a psychological system of sympathetic magic, where preventing an immediate financial drawdown on day one is believed to shield the household from systemic debt over the next twelve months.

Variation and Change: The speaker notes that whereas Saaguvaatu used to be an arduous, multi-day collective labor commitment in the fields, it has now morphed into a brief, highly condensed symbolic act. This tracks the modern shifting of rural labor availability, mechanization, and the partial breakdown of prolonged agrarian ritual frameworks.

Research Note: This narrative is an exceptional find for economic anthropology and folklore studies. It explicitly documents how ecological timing (plowing before Durmuhoortham sets in), natural material selection (babool branches, castor oil, cow dung), and behavioral taboos (monetary freezing) intersect to form a protective canopy over the peasant household at the start of a volatile financial cycle.

Corpus Entry 04: Sivarathri

Festival Name: Sivarathri (Maha Shivaratri)

Speaker: Sathavva

Age: 50

Occupation: Agricultural Labourer

Original Speaker Account:

ఇగ శివరాత్రి పండుగత్తే, ఇగ ఎమన్న రాయేసుని కొంటెతనం, ఎమన్నా కోరుకున్న పనికావలె అది ఇది అంటే నిరారం ఒక్కపోద్దుండి పొద్దుగూకినవలె గింతంత ఏదన్న సత్తసొంటిది చేసుకొని ఇంత ఆడవేట్టుకొని ఇగ ఇంత పువ్వులో, పత్తిరో ఎదో ఎదో జమజేస్కొని ఇగ జాగారం ముట్టిచ్చుకొని పెద్ద రాతిరి దాక అట్ట్నే ఉండి ఇంతంత కొబ్బరికాయ కొట్టి, లేపోతే కొబ్బరికాయకు పైసల్ లేకపోతే ఇంత పప్పున్ను నువ్వులు కలిపి, ఇంతంత శెక్కరి కలిపి, అవ్విలేకపోతే బెల్లమున్ను నువ్వులు కలిపి రాయేసుని పలారముల్లో అని బాజార్ల పంచిపెడుదురు, బజార్ల పంచి పెడ్తే ఆడికి పోరగాండ్లు నాదివేట్టుమని ఈశెయ్యి వాడుదురు మల్ల మావ్వదివెట్టు మని ఈశేయ్యికింద ఈశేయ్యివడుదురు అవ్వి ఇవ్వి ఒక్కండ్లవోసి మల్ల మీదశెయ్యివెట్టి మాతాతదివేట్టువనందురు. గట్ల. నేనైతే కొబ్బరిశిప్పవట్టి చిప్పనింపుకొని అద్దును. మావ్వ పొద్దుగాల తానంజేషి అవ్వివ్వి పనులుసేసినంక, మల్లపోద్దూకి తానంజేసి మా ముసలోనిది పాతదోతి కట్టుకొని జాగారం ముట్టిచ్చి ఒక్కపోద్దిడిషి అదే దోతి మీద కిందవండు. ఇగ సామునముఒక్కపొద్దు, దానికి ఎనుకట దానికి కాలుజేతులు వడిపొయినయట సామునముల ఒక్కపోద్దువట్టి బేడెంచుకుందట పతీసామునముల తానంజేసి ఒక్కపోద్దుండి పొద్దుగూకి ఐదిండ్లు, గిదే! నల్లదదే దోతి మడికట్టుకొని పాతదోతి సంకజోలె కట్టుకోని ఐదిండ్లు రాయేసుని బుచ్చం అని జోలేత్తుకొని ఆ బియ్యం దేవుడచ్చేటోల్లకి ఇచ్చచ్చు. ఎనుకట జాగారం అంటే బాగా బక్తి ఉండే ఇప్పుడు సేపులు తినుడు అంగుర్లు తినుడు అవ్వితినుడు ఇవ్వితినుడు ఎదో ఉన్నం అంటే ఉండుడన్నట్టు ఒక్కపొద్దు ఉండుడు గని ఏమిలేదు.

English Translation: When the Sivarathri festival arrives, if we desire any playful blessing from Lord Shiva (Raayesu), or if we want a specific wish fulfilled, we observe a strict waterless fast (Niraaram Okkapoddu). Once night falls (Poddugookinavale), we prepare a small meal or some sago-like gruel (Satthasontidhi), place it before the deity, gather some flowers and leaves (Patthiro), and light the sacred vigil lamp (Jaagaaram mutthicchukoni). We stay awake until late into the night (Pedda Raathiri). We then crack a small piece of coconut. If we don’t have money for a coconut, we mix roasted lentils and sesame seeds with a little sugar; or if that is unavailable, we mix jaggery and sesame seeds. We distribute this in the streets, calling out, "Here is Lord Shiva’s festive offering (Raayesuni Palaaramullo)!" When we distribute it in the streets, the local children gather, holding out one hand, begging, "Put it in mine!" Then, they stretch another hand beneath the first, shouting, "Put it in my mother’s share!" They gather these portions together, place another hand on top, and plead, "Put some for my grandfather too." That’s how it was. As for me, I would hold a half-coconut shell, fill it up, and serve them. My grandmother (Maavva) would bathe in the morning, finish her chores, and bathe again at sunset. She would then wear my grandfather's (Maamusoloni) old dhoti, light the vigil lamp, break her fast, and sleep on the bare floor wrapped in that very same dhoti. Then there is the Somavaaram (Monday) fast; they say in the past, her limbs had grown weak, but by holding the Monday fast, her health improved. Every Monday, she would bathe, fast until sunset, and then, wearing that same ritually pure (Madi) dark dhoti and carrying an old dhoti tied into a shoulder-bag (Sanka Jole), she would visit five houses. Holding up the bag, she would beg for alms, saying, "Alms in the name of Lord Shiva (Raayesuni Buccham)!" and would later give that collected rice to the wandering ascetics who came to the village. In the old days, the night vigil meant intense, deep devotion. Now, it’s just about eating potato chips, grapes, and munching on this and that. It is as if we are just existing for the sake of it; there is fasting, but the true spirit is gone.

Festival Practices: Observing a waterless fast (Niraaram Okkapoddu); preparing Satthasontidhi (sago porridge/frugal offering); gathering wild flowers and leaves (Patthiro); lighting the ritual night-vigil lamp (Jaagaaram mutthicchu); performing animal/coconuts offerings or cost-effective alternative offerings (sesame seeds mixed with lentils, jaggery, or sugar); communal distribution of Palaaram (offerings) to neighborhood children; complex intersectional rituals where women wear their deceased/elderly husband's old dhotis as a ritual purity garment (Madi); sleeping on the bare floor; observing lifelong Somavaaram (Monday) vows for physical healing; algorithmic begging of raw rice (Buccham) from exactly five households (Aidhindlu) using a cloth shoulder-bag (Sanka Jole); and dedicating the collected grains to visiting religious ascetics (Devudacchetoallu).

Local Terminology:

Raayesdu (రాయేసుడు): A distinct, deeply localized subaltern phonetic corruption of Rajeshwarudu (Lord Shiva of Vemulawada) or Rayalingadu, reflecting an intimate, non-Sanskritized kinship with the deity.

Niraaram (నిరారం): Regional dialectal contraction of Niraahaaram (completely empty-stomach / waterless fast).

Satthasontidhi (సత్తసొంటిది): Refers to Satthu or sago-based, low-cost gruel specifically consumed to sustain energy during religious fasts.

Patthiro (పత్తిరో): Localized form of Patram (sacred leaves/Bilva leaves used for Shiva worship).

Jaagaaram mutthicchu (జాగారం ముట్టిచ్చు): The phrase changes the abstract noun 'vigil' (Jagaranam) into a concrete action of "igniting the vigil light," indicating the physical lighting of the ritual lamp that anchors the night-long awake state.

Buccham / Bucchaalu (బుచ్చం): Dialectal variant of Bhiksham (ritual alms/begging for sacred fulfillment).

Madi (మడి): Ritual purity enforced through strict codes of washing and isolating garments used exclusively for sacred tasks.

Cultural Interpretation: Sathavva’s narrative provides raw, unmediated text on how subaltern women navigate poverty, resource scarcity, and bodily health through personalized religious vows. The substitution of a coconut with a mixture of sesame seeds, lentils, and jaggery (pappunnu nuvvulu) reveals a highly adaptive ritual economy. When financial capital is absent, folklore allows the peasant to substitute expensive commodities with everyday kitchen staples without losing ritual validity.

The practice of the grandmother wearing the Maamusoloni (old man's) dhoti as a Madi garment exposes a fascinating gender and kinship dynamic. In these marginalized settings, specialized silk Madi clothes are non-existent; instead, an elderly husband’s old cotton dhoti is repurposed as an instrument of ascetic purity. The specific performative structure of children stacking their hands (eesheyyi kinda eesheyyi) to claim shares for absent ancestors illustrates that the street distribution of Palaaram functions as an informal system of community care and ancestral remembrance.

Variation and Change: The speaker sets up a sharp contrast between the past (Enukata) and the present, critiquing the contemporary commercialization of the sacred vigil. The transition from intense bodily devotion and sleeping on bare floors to the casual consumption of modern snack foods ("chips and grapes") is framed as a loss of authentic spiritual substance. The ritual has transformed from an active, body-disciplining ascetic trial into a passive, consumerist leisure event.

Research Note: This text is structurally rich for research in gendered anthropology and subaltern religious studies. It details a specific healing narrative where a woman remedies physical paralysis (kaalujaethulu vadipoyinayi) through the Somavaaram vow, converting her recovered mobility into structured, ritualized begging (Sanka Jole) across five specific houses to feed wandering ascetics. It captures how vulnerable bodies use folk-religious vows to build social agency, physical resilience, and networks of localized charity.

Corpus Entry 05: Sankuraathri

Festival Name: Sankuraathri / Katrevula Panduga (Makar Sankranti & Cattle Rituals)

Speaker: Sudhayya

Age: 53

Occupation: Farmer

Original Speaker Account:

సంకురాత్రి అచ్చిందంటే మన సంపర్దాయం పకారం పిండికురాలు అండుకోవాలే, తీపిజేస్కోవలె అంటే, ఇక్కడ గుద్దవలగా కోస్కతిన్దురు. కోన్ని కోస్కుందురు అందరు , అందరుగవ్వే పులుసో – బుట్టనో నాకుదురు. పొద్దుగాల లేషి ఇల్లలికి కడుపలకు, అరుగులకు, మొగురాలకు జాజువూసి. గిరుకతోని గీతల ముగ్గులు ఇల్లంతేత్తురు. ఇంటిముంగట ముగ్గేసి అండ్ల గొబ్బెమ్మలువెట్టి ధాన్యాలు, నేరగాయలు ఎత్తురు. ఆంధ్రకేయ్యేమో మంచిపండుగా అని మంచిగ మొక్కుతే, ఈళ్ళు పీడపండుగు ఆ పండుగీపండుగని ఈల్లేమో దానిమీదికేల్లి వోవాలె దీనిమీదికేల్లి వోవాలని ఒకనాల్గు రోజులముందు కాట్రేవుల పండుగ అని చేద్దురు. కాట్రేవుల పండుగ అంటే, ఎనుకట ఊల్లె ఎడ్లు, గొడ్లు, బర్ర్లఅన్ని ఒక్కమోకేయ్యి కొట్టుకపోయ్యి కాసుకద్దురు ఎనుకట. కాటిదేవునికి ఏందంటే, ఒక్కపోద్దుండి బెల్లబువ్వండి కల్లు అవన్నీ మిరుపకాయ దొండకాయ ఆన్ని ఏకూరగాయలుంటే ఆకురగాయలు దండలుగుచ్చి మావిడి కొమ్మలుగుచ్చి ఓకాడ దోరణంగట్టి అక్కడికిక్కడికి ఆయింత ఈ ఒక్కపొద్దున్నొడు అవిట్టెంబడి, ఆ బర్లుగొడ్ల ఎంబడి సుట్టు ఉరుకుతే ఓలొట్టి, ఉడుకుడుకు బువ్వ, ముద్దలుతోని, ఆఒక్కపొద్దున్నొడు ఆ ఎడ్లగొడ్లసుట్టు ఉరుకంగ ఈ ఉడుకుముద్దలు వట్టుకొని, బరివాతనే ఉండాలే ఈ ఒక్కపొద్దున్నోడు. వాడు ఉర్రుకుతే ఆ ఉడుకుడుకుముద్దలు దొప్పల వట్టుకొని ఈపుల సంపుతే వావ్వ - వాయ్యా అనుకుంట ఉరుకు. అది కాట్రేవుల పండుగ అందురు. ఎగవడి ఉరికి ఒకోమ్ముకు సంపినవలె ఇగ ఎగవడి మంద ఇడిశినవలే ఇగ ఆవిటెంబడివడి ఊల్లెకిఅత్తుము. ఊల్లె సోచ్చినంక అవ్విటికీ జాజో కలర్లో అవ్విటిపెయ్యికి కొట్టుదుము.

English Translation: When Sankuraathri arrives, according to our tradition (Sampardaayam), we must cook fried/sweet flour savories (Pindikuraalu). If we want to make it sweet, here people slaughter and eat a sheep or goat (Guddavalaga kosi thindhuru). Everyone slaughters a fowl and tastes the meat curry (Pulso) or the seasoned rice (Butthano). Waking up early in the morning, we mop the house (Illaliki), smearing red mud (Jaaju) onto the doorsteps, verandas, and the bases of the pillars (Moguraalu). Using a rolling stencil tool (Giruka), we decorate the entire house with geometric chalk lines. In front of the house, we draw a large design, place Gobbemmalu (cow-dung balls) in it, and scatter grains and seasonal wild berries (Naeragaayalu). While the coastal Andhra people treat this as a highly auspicious festival and worship devoutly, the local people here look at it as a time of shifting away misfortune (Peeda Panduga). To ward off evils and clear the paths, they celebrate Kaatrevula Panduga about four days prior. In the old days, for Kaatrevula Panduga, they would gather all the village oxen, sheep, and buffaloes into a single massive herd and drive them to the forest clearing. For the deity of the wilderness (Kaati Devudu), the one observing the fast cooks sweet jaggery-rice (Bellambuwwa) and offers toddy (Kallu). They string together whatever vegetables are available—chillies, ivy gourds—along with mango leaves, and tie them as a festive arch (Dhoranam) across the path. The person observing the fast must run stark naked (Barivaathana) along with the cattle, encircling the entire herd. While he runs around the oxen and buffaloes, others carry small leaf-bowls (Doppala) filled with steaming hot rice balls (Uduku muddhalu) and forcefully pelt them onto his bare back. As they strike his back, he runs crying out in pain, "Wavva! Vayya!" That is what they call Kaatrevula Panduga. Running forward with great force, once the hot rice strikes the cattle's horns and the herd is unleashed, we chase them back into the village. Once they enter the village, we paint their bodies with red mud (Jaaju) or vibrant colors.

Festival Practices: Preparing traditional fried pastries (Pindikuraalu); ritual slaughter of livestock (Guddavalaga) and poultry; cleaning and smearing the house with cow-dung; painting doorsteps and architectural pillars with red earth (Jaaju); applying geometric chalk patterns using a mechanical roller (Giruka); placing cow-dung mounds (Gobbemmalu) with grains and wild berries (Naeragaayalu) at the threshold; observing Kaatrevula Panduga (worship of the forest protector deity Kaati Devudu) to ward off village misfortunes (Peeda); gathering the entire settlement's livestock into a singular collective herd; preparing ritual jaggery-rice (Bellambuwwa) and fermenting liquor (Kallu) offerings; constructing a defensive vegetative archway (Dhoranam) using threaded vegetables (chillies, ivy gourds) and mango leaves; a male ritual actor running completely naked (Barivaathana) around the running herd; ritual pelting of boiling-hot rice balls (Uduku muddhalu) from leaf-bowls onto the bare back of the naked runner; unleashing the stampeding herd back into the settlement; and painting the hides of the cattle with Jaaju or synthetic colors upon re-entry.

Local Terminology:

Pindikuraalu (పిండిкуరాలు): Regional terms for deep-fried or steamed flour-based festive snacks and savories.

Guddavalaga (గుద్దవలగా): A highly specific dialectal expression denoting a sheep or goat selected for slaughter.

Butthano (బుట్టనో): A local term for seasoned, compressed festive rice or a specific preparation of meat-infused rice.

Moguraalu (మొగురాలకు): The structural wooden or stone pillars that support the roof of traditional rural Telangana houses.

Jaaju (జాజు): A natural, iron-rich red oxide earth used to paint borders on thresholds and walls during protective rituals.

Giruka (గిరుక): A specialized, rolling hollow tube tool with punctured patterns used to rapidly roll out complex geometric powder designs on the floor.

Peeda Panduga (పీడపండుగు): "The Festival of Warding Off Afflictions/Evils," showing a stark theological difference from typical textbook definitions of Sankranti.

Kaati Devudu / Kaatrevu (కాటిదేవునికి): The fierce guardian deity of the village boundaries, wilderness, and pastures, responsible for shielding livestock from epidemics and wildlife predators.

Barivaathana (బరివాతనే): Completely naked / devoid of any apparel.

Doppala (దొప్పల): Leaf-bowls stitched together from dry Banyan or Palasa leaves, used to hold hot ritual food.

Cultural Interpretation: Sudhayya’s narrative provides brilliant ethnographic data on how agrarian communities conceptualize boundary maintenance, animal health, and regional identity. His direct contrast between Coastal Andhra practice ("Andhrastole... manchi panduga") and Telangana practice ("Peeda Panduga") shows how the subaltern communities of Telangana view Sankranti. Instead of framing it purely as a joyous solar harvest festival, the local memory views it as a critical, high-risk transition period where malignant spiritual entities and diseases (Peeda) must be physically and ritually driven away from the settlement's primary capital: its livestock.

The shocking ritual of Kaatrevula Panduga contains deep elements of sympathetic magic and ritual scapegoating. A designated community member must shed his apparel (Barivaathana), casting off his social identity to walk among the animal herd. Pelting his bare back with boiling-hot rice balls (Uduku muddhalu) acts as a bodily transfer of heat, inflammation, and disease vectors out of the cattle herd and onto the human scapegoat. The vegetable archway (Dhoranam) acts as a protective energetic filter: as the animals stampede under the threaded chillies and bitter gourds, any attached evil eye or epidemic pathogen is symbolically sliced away.

Variation and Change: The speaker repeatedly inserts the marker Enukata (in the old days) to describe the collective driving of the entire village's cattle into a singular herd. This highlights a shift from historical communal pasture management and shared ritual labor to a highly individualized, fragmented contemporary setup where these complex, high-discipline public body rituals have mostly disappeared.

Corpus Entry 06: Dasara

Festival Name: Dasara (Vijayadashami)

Speaker: Narsayya

Age: 84

Occupation: Farmer

Original Speaker Account:

మక్కలు ఎంచి సత్హు చేస్కుందురు బియ్యం ఎంచి సత్హు చేస్కుందురు తెల్లజోన్న్లు ఎంచి సత్తు చేస్కుందురు. ఒక్క దసరకు కొన్న చీరే నాలుగైదు దసరలకి కట్టుకున్డుము పైసల్లెక. బిన్ను జాకిట్లువట్టిచ్చుకుంటే పెద్ద, నేశిన కనుములేమో చిన్న. వంటలేమో ఉన్నోల్లేమో ఉన్నతీరు లేనోల్లేమో లేనోల్లెతీరు చేస్కతింద్దురు. జొన్నలు, మక్కలు పదనజేసి బెట్ట్రిచ్చి ఎండవోసి మల్ల నానవోసి వడవోసి దంచి సాకినాలు గారప్పలు చేస్కుందుము. రెండుమూడోద్దుల ముందాలనే బెట్లిచ్చి, ఎండవోత్తుము. ఎండవోసి మల్ల పోటువేట్టుడుము. తెల్లగా బియ్యమోలె పోటువెట్టి దంచుదుము దంచి చేసుకుందుము. ఇగ పండుగ నాడు కోడినో మేకనో కోసుకొని ఇన్ని గుడాలు పోసుకొని తిన్దుము. పైసలున్నోడు మేకనుకోసుకును పైసల్లెనోడు కోడినో – బుట్టనో కోసుకొని ఒడ్దేకిచ్చుకును. ఇగ జంబి, కొన్నక్కల్ల పొద్దుగాలనే కొడుదురు. పొద్దుగాలనే జంబి పిల్లలచ్చు అప్పుడు దొరలున్నపుడు ఎట్టి పిల్లలచ్చు. సాకలోడు కొట్టేటోడు, తేనుగోడు బోనం అన్దేటోడు. దేవునికాడికిపొయి సారుగమ్మ కాడికివొయ్యి పూదిచ్చి, పోగేసి, పిల్లకు బొట్టువెట్టి జెడ్తిచ్చినంక ఆయింత అటోగలు ఇటోగలువట్టి సోప్పకట్ట వెట్టి పిల్లనిగొట్టి జమ్బాకు తెంపుకోని అత్తురు. ఇంటికచ్చి జమ్మి ఆకు ఇంట్ల పెద్దోళ్లకి చేతులవెట్టి కాళ్ళు మొక్కీ దీవెనలు తీసుకొందురు. ఎవడన్న పగున్నోడు అక్కన్నే లోల్లివేట్టుకొన్ని కొట్టుకుందురు.

English Translation: We would select corn, rice, or white sorghum to prepare the roasted flour mixture (Satthu). Due to the lack of money, a saree bought for one Dasara would be worn for four or five subsequent Dasara festivals. If we got blouses tailored, they would be oversized, but the woven borders (Kanumulu) were always too small. As for the festive cooking, the rich ate according to their wealth, and the poor managed with whatever little they had. We would soak sorghum and corn until they reached the right moisture level (Padana), let them sprout slightly (Betthricchi), dry them in the sun, then soak them again, drain them, and pound them to make Sakinalu and Garappalu. Two or three days in advance, we would let the grains sprout and dry them. After drying, we would pound them into a fine flour (Potu). We would pound them until the flour looked as white as polished rice, and then prepare the snacks. On the festival day, we would slaughter a fowl or a goat and eat it along with boiled spiced legumes (Gudaalu). Those with money would slaughter a goat, while those without money would slaughter a fowl or a small bird and manage to pull through (Oddekicchukunu). The ritual cutting of the Jambi (Shami tree branches) used to happen early in the morning. Back then, during the era of the feudal landlords (Doralu), the Jambi branches were brought down through forced caste labor (Etti). The washerman (Saakalodu) was the one who chopped it, and the toddy-tapper (Thaenugodu) was responsible for handling the food offerings (Bonam). They would go to the deity, visit the village border goddess (Saarugamma), offer incense, build a ritual mound, apply a tilak to the branch, and after performing the sacrifice (Jedthicchinanka), men from both sides would hold dried millet stalks (Soppakatta), strike the branch, pluck the Jambi leaves, and return home. They give the jambi leaves to the elders in their home and take blessings. If there was any pre-existing enmity between groups, they would start a massive brawl and fight right there at the ritual site.

Festival Practices: Hand-selecting local grains (corn, rice, white sorghum) to prepare ritual Satthu flour; multi-day processing of grain involving moisture balancing (Padana), sprouting (Betthricchi), sun-drying, and pounding (Potu) to bake Sakinalu and Garappalu; animal or poultry sacrifice based on household economic stratification; preparation of Gudaalu (boiled legumes); early morning Jambi (Jammichettu/Shami tree) worship driven by Etti (feudal forced labor); hierarchical division of ritual duties based on sub-castes (Washerman/Chakali chops the wood, Tenugu/Toddy-tapper handles the Bonam); worshiping the village border deity (Saarugamma); ritual flagellation of the sacred branch using millet stalks (Soppakatta); collective plucking of Shami leaves ("gold"); and venting hidden structural factionalism or interpersonal enmities through physical brawls at the boundary site.

Local Terminology:

Satthu (సత్హు/సత్తు): A traditional energy-dense festive powder made of roasted, pounded grains mixed with jaggery.

Kanumulu (కనుములేమో): The distinct woven borders or selvages of handloom fabrics and traditional sarees.

Padana (పదనజేసి): The precise level of optimal moisture or dampness required in grains before processing.

Betthricchi (బెట్ట్రిచ్చి/బెట్లిచ్చి): A distinct regional agricultural term meaning to allow soaked grains to split or slightly germinate under controlled conditions.

Potu (పోటువేట్టుడుము): The heavy, rhythmic pounding of grains using a massive wooden pestle (Onka) in a stone mortar to achieve ultra-fine flour.

Oddekicchukunu (ఒడ్దేకిచ్చుకును): A deep Telangana idiomatic expression meaning to somehow stay afloat, survive a crisis, or successfully manage a hard situation despite minimal resources.

Jambi (జంబి): The Shami tree (Prosopis cineraria), central to Vijayadashami folklore as a symbol of victory, prosperity, and structural rejuvenation.

Etti (ఎట్టి): A brutal historical system of unpaid, caste-enforced feudal labor extracted by Doralu (landlords) in Telangana.

Jedthicchinanka (జెడ్తిచ్చినంక): A dialectal variant meaning to deliver a strike, finish a sacrifice, or complete a ritual severing.

Soppakatta (సోప్పకట్ట): A tied bundle of dry sorghum or millet stalks used as fodder or, in this context, as a ritual hitting tool.

Cultural Interpretation: Narsayya’s narrative offers a raw look at how class boundaries and feudal politics heavily shaped the celebration of Dasara in historical Telangana. His memories strip away the romanticized view of rural harmony by directly mapping economic disparity onto the festive kitchen ("unnoallemo unnathiru, laenoallemo laenoallu"). For poor peasants, celebrating meant stretching a single festive garment across five years and using semantic resilience (Oddekicchukunu) to survive the financial strain of the holiday.

The description of the Jambi ritual exposes the intersection of caste power and folk religion. Under the Dora (landlord) regime, sacred celebrations were fueled by Etti (forced labor), where lower-caste bodies were assigned fixed, unequal tasks based on sub-caste hierarchies (Saakalodu for chopping, Thaenugodu for carrying food). The physical layout of the ritual reflects this politics: the Jambi must be processed outside the village core at the shrine of Saarugamma (the border goddess). This boundary site represents a high-stress zone where hidden social tensions overflow. The final act of plucking the leaves often devolved into a literal brawl ("lolli vettukoni kottukundhuru"), showing that the sacred site functioned as an outlet for structural grievances and village factionalism.

Variation and Change: The 84-year-old speaker paints a clear picture of change by contrasting the historical Dora era of forced labor (Etti) with contemporary times. Furthermore, the hand-crafted, tedious culinary art of preparing Shaamala Paasham (manually rolling Errallu threads) has been replaced by instant, store-bought commercial packaged vermicelli. This tracks the loss of pre-industrial domestic labor traditions and food patterns in Telangana.

It perfectly captures how marginal communities weaponize folk superstitions to legalize temporary property damage and interpersonal warfare (Bandaleyyudu), archiving a crucial, non-Sanskritized model of festive conflict management [INDEX].

Corpus Entry 10: Holi / Kaamuni Panduga

Festival Name: Holi / Kaamuni Panduga (Kaama Dahanam)

Speaker: Mahender

Age: 45

Occupation: Police Constable (Formerly from a rural background)

Original Speaker Account:

కామునిపండుగా.. హోలీ అంటే కామునిపండుగు ఆప్పట్ల. కాముడాడుదురు, కామదానంజెయ్యాలంటే పిలగాళ్లందరు కొలలాడుదురు, ఆడోల్లు సప్పట్లుగొట్టుకుంట కాముడాడుదురు. ఇండ్లపోన్న తోరుగుదురు, ఇంత మక్కలో, బియ్యమో, పప్పో, పైసలో ఇద్దురు ఎవలన్న. అవ్వివట్టుకొని కోమట్లింటికోయ్యి అవ్విటికీ పైసలిత్తె పైసలు లేకపోతే ఎమన్న కొనుక్కతిందురు. ఇగ ఆకరినాడు ఆకోలలు కచ్చిర్కాడ కామదికాడ అనుంట్టుండే, ఇగ అందరు ఆడుకొని ఎవలుముందత్తే ఆల్లు కాముడు అంట్టువెట్టి ఆకోలలు అన్డ్లేద్దురు. అన్డ్లేసి కాలవెట్టి ఇంటికద్దురు. కామదానం అంటదే. ఇగ తెల్లారి రంగులువుసుకొని హోలీ ఆడుతరు. అది మొన్నమోన్నచ్చిందట మా తాతన్నడు.

English Translation: Kaamuni Panduga... back in those days, Holi was called Kaamuni Panduga. They would play Kaamudu. To perform the ritual burning of the God of Desire (Kaama Dahanam), young boys would play a rhythmic stick-dance (Kolalaadudhuru), while the women would dance and clap their hands (Sappatlu gottukunta). They would wander from house to house; people would give them some corn, rice, lentils, or coins. Taking these items, they would visit the merchant’s (Komati) shop; the merchant would either exchange those grains for cash or give them snacks to eat. On the final day, those dancing sticks were stacked at the village square (Kacchiri) where the Kaamudu pyre was built. After everyone finished dancing, whoever arrived first would ignite the Kaamudu pyre and toss those dancing sticks into the fire. After burning it completely, they would return home. That is what Kaama Dahanam means. The next morning, they would smear colors onto each other and play Holi. My grandfather used to say that this practice of playing with colors arrived only very recently.

Festival Practices: Rhythmic folk stick-dancing (Kolalu) by young boys; organized clapping circle-dances (Kaamudaadu) by village women; door-to-door resource pooling (collecting corn, rice, lentils, and small coins); traditional barter or transactional conversion of gathered grains at the merchant's (Komati) shop for festive sweets or cash; gathering at the communal village square (Kacchiri); constructing a public pyre for Kaama Dahanam; competitive torching of the pyre; burning the dancing sticks as part of the sacrificial flame; and playing with colors on the subsequent morning.

Local Terminology:

Kaamuni Panduga / Kaamudaadu (కామునిపండుగా/కాముడాడుదురు): The authentic, localized non-Sanskritized name for the festival, centering the narrative entirely on Kamadeva (the God of Desire) and regional folklore rather than the North Indian Holika myth.

Kolalaadudhuru (కొలలాడుదురు): A rhythmic folk performance using wooden sticks (Kolalu) struck against each other, traditionally performed by village youths during the counting days leading up to the bonfire.

Komati (కోమట్లు): The local trading and grocery-vending caste, functioning as the foundational micro-bank and grain-exchange node in the village economy.

Kacchiri (కచ్చిర్కాడ): The historical village square, open-air community court, or the platform outside the village headman's house where public assemblies and high-status rituals occur.

Anttuwetti (అంట్టువెట్టి): A highly localized dialectal verb meaning to set on fire, ignite, or touch a flame to a pyre.

Monna Monnacchindhata (మొన్నమోన్నచ్చిందట): A distinct idiomatic expression literally meaning "it arrived only the day before yesterday," used here by the speaker to denote a very recent cultural import or a late historical modification of an ancient indigenous ritual.

Cultural Interpretation: Mahender’s field narrative tracks how a festival’s core theological focus can be completely transformed on the ground through subaltern oral memory. Textual Sanskritized Hinduism maps Holi onto the Puranic myth of Prahlada and the burning of the demoness Holika. However, the rural Telangana memory completely bypasses this script. It frames the festival exclusively around Kaamuni Panduga—the destruction and subsequent rebirth of Kamadeva (desire/nature’s fertility).

The economic structure of the festival operates as a highly organized, youth-led communal collection drive. Wandering from house to house (indlaponna thorigedharu), children enforce a temporary festive tax, gathering subsistence grains like corn, rice, and pulses. The transformation of these grains at the Komati (merchant) shop reveals how the village barter economy handled religious events: the local merchant acted as a micro-liquidity pool, converting scattered agrarian crops into immediate festive treats or currency. The climax at the Kacchiri (village square) underscores the communal and spatial nature of the ritual. Burning the very sticks used for the Kolalu dance demonstrates a symbolic sacrifice of the labor and tools used during the preceding days, purifying the village geography before the shift into the new seasonal cycle.

Variation and Change: The 45-year-old speaker introduces an excellent piece of oral historiography by quoting his grandfather (Ma thaathannadu). The grandfather’s memory identifies the modern practice of throwing synthetic wet colors (Rangulu) as a highly superficial, very recent cultural import (monna monnacchindhi). Historically, the festival was strictly an ascetic and dramatic celebration centered around the bonfire, body disciplines, and rhythmic oral songs, rather than the commercialized, color-throwing spectacle seen today.
