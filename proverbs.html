Literal meaning: When they told Malli that her urine works as medicine, she climbed a tamarind tree and sprayed it everywhere.

Themes: Pride, reaction to praise, humour.

Literal Meaning

When repeatedly told, “Your urine has medicinal properties,” she climbed a tamarind tree and splashed it.

Speaker Explanation

“ఒకల్ని కొంచం మెచ్చుకుంటే సాలు, వాళ్ళకి ఎక్కడలేని మిర్ర అత్తది. ఆళ్ళు చేసే పనులకి అద్దు అదుపు ఉండది.”

English Translation

“If you give someone a little praise, they get uncalled-for arrogance. After that, there are no limits or control to the things they do.”

Context of Use

Used during everyday conversations when criticizing someone who behaves in an exaggerated or disproportionate manner after receiving minor recognition.

Themes

Pride, reaction to praise, humour.

Semantic Domain

Bodily waste, local flora, folk beliefs.

Conceptual Mapping

Source Domain: Bodily Excretion (Urine) → Target Domain: Highly Valued Asset / Social Validation.

Figurative Device

Hyperbole, Satire.

Linguistic Features

  • Dialect Lexicon: Features the regional variant మల్లి మల్లి (malli malli - repeatedly) and the low-register colloquial noun ఉచ్చ (ucca - urine).
  • Dialect Morphology: The specific verb చిమ్మిచ్చి (cimmicci) intensifies the action by conveying an energetic, uncontrolled spraying motion.

Research Interpretation

Within this corpus, the proverb appears to function as a critique of excessive pride following minor praise. The speaker uses a stark contrast between a base bodily fluid and a high-climbing act. The linguistic data suggest that the intensifier cimmicci is used to make the target's loss of proportion sound visually ridiculous to listeners, helping the community discourage arrogant behavior through humor.

Literal Meaning

A true housewife supposedly purified her house by mopping it with her own urine.

English Translation

“Some people pretend to be very proper in society. But either to save money or out of sheer stinginess, they do disgusting things.”

Context of Use

Applied when criticizing someone whose excessive stinginess or performative perfectionism leads to a counterproductive, foolish failure.

Themes

Hypocrisy, false virtue, misdirected thrift.

Semantic Domain

Domestic chores, sanitation, ritual purity.

Conceptual Mapping

Source Domain: Domestic Cleansing Routine → Target Domain: Economic Mismanagement / Performative Virtue.

Figurative Device

Paradox, Irony.

Research Interpretation

The data indicate that this proverb serves to expose the conflict between outward social presentation and irrational financial choices. The text relies on a cultural paradox where a cleansing routine is subverted by a crude bodily function. This suggests that the speech community uses the proverb to mock extreme stinginess, showing that obsessive anxiety over resources can lead to foolish behavior.

English Translation

“No matter how much you tell him, he insists that what he says is correct. Even when he knows it is a mistake, he will never admit it.”

Uttered during general disagreements or village arguments to describe a stubborn individual who refuses to back down despite clear proof.

Themes

Obstinacy, ego, denial of truth.

Semantic Domain

Local fauna, hunting, interpersonal disputes.

Conceptual Mapping

Source Domain: Anomalous Animal Anatomy → Target Domain: Psychological Stubbornness / Cognitive Inflexibility.

Hyperbole.

The data suggest that this proverb operates as a linguistic formula to identify and dismiss absolute obstinacy. By reducing a speaker's argument to a biological impossibility (a three-legged rabbit), the proverb provides a non-confrontational way to invalidate an irrational claim. Instead of prolonging an argument, deploying this phrase allows the speech community to collectively flag the individual's stance as an ego-driven error.

Literal meaning: While destiny decreed one should rule a kingdom, bad luck mandated the herding of donkeys.

Themes: Destiny; Misfortune; Wasted potential; Personal choices.

Literal Meaning

While destiny decreed one should rule a kingdom, bad luck mandated the herding of donkeys.

Speaker Explanation

“కొంతమంది రాత ఎట్లుంటదంటే వాళ్ళు అదృష్టం మంచిగుండి పెద్ద పెద్ద నవుకరిల నో, లేక పెద్ద నాయకుడయ్యెటోడు గుడ, వాని చెడ్డ అలవాట్ల తోని గాడిదలు గాయవల్సిన పరిస్థితి తెచ్చుకుంటాడు.”

English Translation

“Some people are fortunate enough to have the potential to secure high-ranking jobs or become important leaders. Yet, because of their own bad habits, they may bring themselves down to a situation where they end up herding donkeys.”

Context of Use

Used when a person with good opportunities or the potential for success ruins their prospects through bad habits, poor choices, or irresponsible behaviour.

Themes

Destiny; Misfortune; Wasted potential; Personal choices.

Semantic Domain

Fate; Occupation; Social status; Human behaviour.

Conceptual Mapping

Source Domain: Being destined to rule a kingdom but ending up herding donkeys.

Target Domain: Losing promising opportunities and social status through one's own behaviour.

Figurative Device

Contrast; Hyperbole; Irony.

Linguistic Features

  • Semantics: The sharp opposition between రాజ్యమేలడం (‘ruling a kingdom’) and గాడిదలు గాయడం (‘herding donkeys’) represents the extreme contrast between potential success and eventual decline.
  • Stylistics: The proverb uses exaggerated social contrast to make the consequences of wasted opportunity vivid and memorable.

Research Interpretation

According to the speaker, the proverb describes a person who has the potential to achieve a high social or professional position but loses that opportunity through bad habits or poor choices. The contrast between ruling a kingdom and herding donkeys creates a striking image of decline from the highest possible status to a lowly occupation. Rather than presenting fate as the only cause of failure, the speaker's explanation places responsibility on the individual's own behaviour, showing how personal choices can undermine favourable circumstances.

Literal meaning: Unsure who is Lord Rama and who is the demon.

Literal Meaning

Unsure who is Lord Rama and who is the demon.

Speaker Explanation

“ఇద్దరు లోల్లివేట్టుకున్నప్పుడు అండ్ల మంచెవడో చెడెవడో మనకు అర్థంగాదు. ఇద్దరు ఒకలేక్కనే కనిపిస్తరు, మనకు లొల్లి మొత్తం ఆగమాగం ఉంటది.”

English Translation

“When two people are fighting, you cannot tell who is good and who is bad among them. Both look the same, and the whole fight is confusing to us.”

Context of Use

Used during general disagreements or family disputes when outside observers cannot figure out who is telling the truth or who is at fault.

Themes

Confusion, moral ambiguity, unresolved conflict.

Semantic Domain

Epic mythology, community disputes.

Conceptual Mapping

Source Domain: Mythological Archetypes (Rama vs. Demon).

Target Domain: Real-world Interpersonal Disputes.

Figurative Device

Allusion.

Research Interpretation

Within this corpus, the proverb appears to function as an expression of situational confusion. By using foundational cultural characters of ultimate good (Rama) and absolute evil (the demon), the phrase highlights a breakdown in simple right-and-wrong judgments during a real-life fight. The data suggest that speakers deploy this phrase to step away from arbitrating a conflict where both sides seem equally deceptive or problematic.

Literal Meaning

Bending down to pluck a brinjal while peeping over to consume a cucumber.

Speaker Explanation

English Translation

“They used to grow Brinjal and cucumbers in one crop back when there were no peacock and birds damaging crops. So, when workers comes to harvest Brinjals they used to sinch and eat Cucumbers by hiding. A person who is stealing walks like a cat. He is completely visible to everyone, but he thinks he is hiding his actions perfectly from others.”

Context of Use

Used to describe clumsy, obvious acts of petty theft or sneaky behavior where the wrongdoer mistakenly believes they are being completely hidden.

Themes

Deception, opportunism, transparency, pettiness.

Semantic Domain

Backyard farming, physical gestures, theft.

Conceptual Mapping

Source Domain: Agricultural Harvesting Postures (Bending/Peeping).

Target Domain: Covert Dishonesty / Sneaky Behavior.

Parallelism, Imagery.

  • Sound Patterns: Built on internal rhythmic rhyme and parallel adverbial participles: వంగి-తెంపి (vaṅgi-tempi - bending and plucking) and తొంగి-తిన్నట్టు (thoṅgi-thinnattu - peeping and eating).
  • Dialect Lexicon: Features regional agricultural verbs like తెంపి (tempi - having plucked).

Research Interpretation

This proverb uses precise physical, kinetic imagery to mock transparent dishonesty. The rhythmic balance of the action words (vaṅgi, tempi, thoṅgi, thinnattu) mirrors the repetitive, habitual nature of sneaky behavior. The data suggest that within closely-knit village communities where everyone watches each other, the proverb functions as a linguistic tool to ridicule people who underestimate the community's collective gaze.

Themes: Fatalism, inevitability of trouble, mortality.

Literal Meaning

If fatal destiny arrives, can thick bushes act as a barrier?

Speaker Explanation

“మనకు సావు గాని, పెద్ద కట్టం గాని రావాలని రాసుంటే మనం ఎక్కడ దాక్కున్నా అది ఆగదు. దాన్నెవ్వరూ ఆపలేరు.”

English Translation

“If death or a massive trouble is destined to come to us, it will not stop no matter where we hide. Nobody can stop it.”

Context of Use

Spoken as a consolatory remark during sudden deaths, natural losses, or unexpected personal disasters to encourage emotional acceptance.

Themes

Fatalism, inevitability of trouble, mortality.

Semantic Domain

Landscape topography, fate, mortality.

Conceptual Mapping

Source Domain: Natural Fencing/Bushes (Podalu) → Target Domain: Defenses against Fate/Death.

Figurative Device

Rhetorical Question.

Linguistic Features

  • Dialect Lexicon: Contains పొదలు (poḍalu), an older regional agrarian noun meaning dense thickets, thick bushes, or wild undergrowth used as natural fencing.
  • Syntax: Formulated as a concise rhetorical question ending in the interrogative clitic అడ్డమా (aḍḍamā - is it an obstacle?), which drives home the point that human barriers are useless against fate.

Research Interpretation

The data indicate that this proverb serves a comforting, structural coping function during times of intense grief or crisis. By framing an unavoidable catastrophe through a rhetorical question, it minimizes individual blame or self-guilt over a bad outcome. The use of the landscape marker poḍalu represents the absolute limit of a villager's physical defenses; showing that even these cannot block fate helps community members process losses by accepting the limits of human control.

Onomatopoeia, Metonymy.

The expression దప్పుడపు నీళ్ళు functions as a metaphor for inadequate effort or excessive economy. According to the speaker, the proverb is no longer restricted to weddings but is extended to any situation in which a task is completed hastily or at the expense of quality. This semantic extension illustrates how culturally specific experiences become generalized expressions of social evaluation.
